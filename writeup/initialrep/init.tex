\section{Introduction}
The pursuit of artificial reverberation has historically been constrained by a 
fundamental dichotomy between acoustic fidelity and computational efficiency,
a dynamic well-defined within the digital signal processing (DSP) domain as 
the quality-efficiency gap. On one end of the continuum, convolution with measured
Room Impulse Responses (RIRs) provides an authentic acoustic fingerprint of a physical space.
Howver, the mathematical necessity of processing extensive arrays of samples results in a
complexity that scales at $O(L \log L)$, where $L$ is the length of the impulse response (IR).
This incurs significant memory bandwidth and latency penalties, rendering convolution unsustainable
for high-density, real-time spatial audio rendering on consumer-grade central processing units
(CPUs).
